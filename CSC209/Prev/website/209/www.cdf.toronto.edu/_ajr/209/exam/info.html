The exam

The exam covers everything in the lectures, assignments, and tutorials, except as excluded below. If you've missed some lectures, meet someone else in the class and get copies of their notes. The exam is "cumulative". The format is much like the midterm, but longer (3 hours allowed, although it oughtn't take most people the full three hours).

All paper "aids" are allowed: books, notes, etc.
No electronic aids are allowed: No computers, calculators, wristwatches which can run unix, electronic diaries, pagers, cell phones, ...

NOTE that the "no electronic aids allowed" rule is a very strict rule. If you take out a calculator or mobile phone or MP3 player, you will be summoned to the Dean's office for an academic offence hearing. Please be careful: either don't bring these devices to the exam at all, or leave them (switched off!) in a backpack or similar at the side or front of the room.

The same goes for any communicating with other students during the exam, even if it's not about course material (there's no way for us to know!). Be careful not to communicate with other students during the exam in any way.

And despite being an open-book exam, most of the other usual restrictions are still in force; for example, you may not have a pencil case at your exam desk. Like any U of T exam, such things also need to go into your backpack or similar, away from your desk.

Valuables in a sufficiently small container, such as a purse, can alternatively be placed on the floor underneath your chair, so long as the container is closed (zipped up), and it's right under your chair, not beside it or under the desk. (I understand that there's some sort of rule at UTM about plastic bags which they supply; I assume you know about this better than I do.)

Exam timetable

Just as with the midterm, you are assigned to a seat; please check your seat number in advance. At the exam, please find the exam label which bears your full name.

The exam will potentially cover:

  • software tools principles, stdin/stdout, inheritance of stdin/out, writing software tools and filters, command-line options, getopt().
  • simple shell programming in "sh": i/o redirection, parentheses, pipes, simple use of variables, use of backquotes, basic commands, control constructs, use of /dev/null
  • the unix filesystem
  • the C programming language (basic syntax, functions, extern, simple printf/scanf, pointers and arrays, simple C preprocessor, argv, strings, files, structs, separate compilation)
  • processes, fork/exec/wait/open/close/dup2, pipe()
  • C memory allocation ("malloc"), C data types
  • inter-process communication: signals, sockets, TCP/IP, network byte order and network newline convention
  • process states (blocked versus ready to run)
  • all of the assignments and tutorials, excluding tutorial 3 as well as the 'Makefile' bit in tutorial 4
I do mean "potentially" -- obviously a three-hour exam can't be comprehensive over this range of material.

Other matters which are in the course (to varying extents) but are excluded from exam coverage:

  • syntax regarding how to write a man page, and list of sections (but you do have to be able to write a 'usage' message in a C program or shell script)
  • "Makefile" syntax
  • other TCP services; X windows connection mechanisms
  • details about memory management, the process table and such, and context switching (multitasking implementation)
  • unix and internet security
  • concurrency stuff; Dijkstra's P and V; the "dining philosophers" problem; deadlock
  • posix threads

Problems
